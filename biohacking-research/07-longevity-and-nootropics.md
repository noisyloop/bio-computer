# 07 — Longevity & Nootropics

> **Two things wearing one name.** The entire value of this file is holding apart (a) legitimate aging biology and (b) the supplement/protocol/influencer market that borrows its vocabulary. `[commerce]` tags mark the second. Pairs with `06`.

## What it is

**Longevity** covers two disjoint activities that share a word:

**(a) Geroscience — the real research field.** Aging biology has matured into a serious discipline organized around identified mechanisms ("hallmarks of aging": genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alteration, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem-cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication, and later additions). Real, funded, peer-reviewed work targets these: **senolytics** (drugs clearing senescent cells), **partial epigenetic reprogramming** (Yamanaka-factor work to reset cellular age, e.g. Sinclair-lab and Altos Labs efforts), **mTOR inhibition** (rapamycin as a candidate geroprotector), **NAD+ metabolism**, and the search for aging biomarkers/"clocks" (Horvath epigenetic clock). Some interventions have strong animal evidence; human evidence lags and is the honest bottleneck. This is legitimate science with the normal caveat that lifespan-extension results in mice have a poor track record of translating to humans.

**(b) The longevity/wellness market.** A large `[commerce]` industry sells supplements (NMN, resveratrol, spermidine, etc.), testing subscriptions, "age reversal" protocols, and personal-brand optimization regimes, drafting on (a)'s credibility. The evidence for most consumer longevity supplements in humans ranges from thin to absent; the marketing implies far more than the trials support. Bryan Johnson's "Blueprint" is the emblematic modern case — a heavily documented, expensive personal-optimization protocol turned brand/product line: genuinely rigorous in self-measurement, but n=1, self-promotional, and now selling supplements, so **`[commerce]`**-adjacent and to be read as such.

**Nootropics** ("smart drugs"/cognitive enhancers) run the same split. The evidenced end is narrow: caffeine (+L-theanine), and prescription stimulants/modafinil that work but carry real trade-offs and aren't "free lunch" enhancement. Most of the "nootropic stack" supplement market (racetams, herbal blends, proprietary formulas) has weak, inconsistent, or absent human evidence for cognitive benefit in healthy people, plus the same publication-bias and placebo problems as DIY tDCS (`05`). The honest summary: sleep, exercise, and not being stressed beat almost every nootropic, and the supplement market monetizes the hope that a pill can substitute.

## Current state (2026)

**The science is real and accelerating; the human payoff is still unproven.** Geroscience is well-funded (Altos, Hevolution, Calico) and mechanistically rich; senolytics and partial reprogramming are the hottest threads, and aging "clocks" are improving as biomarkers. But the field's honesty test is translation, and as of 2026 there is still **no proven human anti-aging intervention** beyond the boring basics (don't smoke, exercise, sleep, don't be obese) — rapamycin and metformin remain candidates under study (TAME's status is the bellwether), not established geroprotectors for healthy people. [verify TAME and senolytic-trial status.]

**The consumer longevity/supplement market is booming and mostly ahead of its evidence.** NMN and resveratrol supplements sell heavily on animal data and mechanistic stories, not human outcome trials; the "age reversal" framing outruns what's shown. Nootropics similarly: the market is large, the healthy-person cognitive-enhancement evidence stays weak. The gap between (a) and (b) is, if anything, widening as the science gets more credible (lending borrowed authority to the marketing) — which makes the distinguish-primary-from-marketing discipline more important, not less.

## Red flags / caveats

- **Mouse ≠ human.** Lifespan extension in model organisms has a long history of not translating. Discount any "reverses aging" claim not backed by human outcome data.
- **Mechanism story ≠ outcome evidence.** "Activates sirtuins / raises NAD+ / clears senescent cells" describes a mechanism, not a proven benefit. The supplement market sells mechanism stories as if they were results.
- **Scientist-founder entanglement.** Several leading researchers have commercial stakes (Sinclair, Longo). Their science can be excellent *and* their promotion self-interested — hold both; read the paper, not the podcast.
- **n=1 spectacle (Blueprint).** Rigorous self-measurement by one wealthy person is a data point and a brand, not evidence a protocol works or is worth its cost. `[commerce]`.
- **Supplement quality/safety.** Unregulated purity, dosing, and long-term-safety unknowns (especially chronic high-dose novel compounds). "Natural/supplement" ≠ safe or studied.
- **Nootropics: sleep/exercise dominate.** Almost no supplement beats fixing the basics; the market monetizes the wish that one could.
- **Regulatory arbitrage** — "supplement" (vs. drug) status lets products make suggestive claims without drug-level evidence. Treat that framing as a red flag, not a reassurance.
