# 01 — DIY Bio & Citizen Science

## What it is

DIY bio (a.k.a. biohacking in its original, most literal sense, or "garage biology") is the application of hacker/maker culture to molecular biology: doing real wet-lab work — cloning, PCR, bacterial transformation, cell-free protein synthesis, bioinformatics — outside traditional institutions. The movement crystallized around 2008–2010 with the founding of DIYbio.org and the first community labs, driven by falling costs of lab equipment (often bought used or built open-source) and gene synthesis. The core idea: molecular biology should be accessible to amateurs the way computing became accessible to hobbyists in the 1970s–80s.

The center of gravity is the **community lab** — a membership-based shared wet lab (BSL-1, occasionally BSL-2) where hobbyists, students, artists, and startups rent bench access. These are real functioning labs with safety norms, not metaphors. The work ranges from educational (learning to pipette, glowing bacteria) to genuinely novel (biosensors, fermentation, bioart, small biotech startups incubated on shared benches). Much of it overlaps with the academic synthetic-biology world, especially through **iGEM**, the international student synthetic-biology competition, which functions as a talent and norms pipeline.

A distinct and more controversial wing is **self-experimental / at-home genetic engineering** — selling kits to do bacterial CRISPR at your kitchen table, and at the extreme, people attempting gene therapy on themselves. This wing (The ODIN, Josiah Zayner) is where DIY bio collides with bioethics, FDA jurisdiction, and biosafety debate.

Biosafety and biosecurity self-governance is a defining feature. The community wrote its own codes of conduct early (Ask a Biosafety Expert, the DIYbio codes from the 2011 congresses) partly to preempt the "bioterror in a garage" narrative. In practice the scene skews heavily toward BSL-1 organisms and educational work; the gap between the scary framing and the actual risk is large, and worth understanding if you're evaluating the field's substance.

## Current state (2026)

Genuinely alive. The community-lab model persists — Genspace, BioCurious, Counter Culture Labs are long-running institutions (10+ years), which is itself a signal of substance. The scene matured past its 2010–2014 hype peak into something quieter and more real: education, small biotech incubation, bioart, and a steady iGEM pipeline. [verify: current operating status of each named lab — some community labs did not survive COVID-era disruptions.]

What faded: the breathless "anyone will engineer life in their garage" media narrative, and the more reckless self-experimentation stunts (which got attention but didn't scale into a movement). What's durable: the labs, the norms, the iGEM/academia bridge, and open-source tooling (OpenTrons-style automation, open lab hardware).

The biosecurity conversation has intensified with cheaper gene synthesis and better AI-assisted design tools, so expect more regulatory attention to DNA-synthesis screening — a policy thread more than a community one (see `08`).

## Red flags / caveats

- **Self-experimentation ≠ the movement.** Zayner-style self-injection stunts get outsized coverage but represent a tiny, contested fringe. Don't take them as representative of community bio, which is overwhelmingly BSL-1 educational/hobbyist work.
- **Kit marketing.** At-home CRISPR kits are real but their capabilities are modest (bacterial/yeast edits); marketing can imply more than the biology delivers. `[commerce]` where kits are sold.
- **"Curing X at home" claims** — DIY gene-therapy self-trials are ethically and medically fraught, unvalidated, and occasionally dangerous. Treat any such claim as anecdote, not evidence.
- **Bioterror framing overcorrection.** The scare narrative is largely overblown for the current community, but the underlying biosecurity concern about cheap synthesis is legitimate at the technology level — don't dismiss it just because the "garage bioterrorist" trope is lazy.
- **Survivorship in the org list** — community labs open and close; verify any given lab still operates before treating it as current.
