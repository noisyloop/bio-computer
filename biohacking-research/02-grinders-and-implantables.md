# 02 — Grinders & Implantables

## What it is

"Grinders" are practical, DIY-oriented body-modification enthusiasts who implant technology into themselves — the term comes from the transhumanist/comic-adjacent milieu (grinding = incremental self-augmentation with available parts). Where academic sensory-augmentation research (`03`) uses grants and IRBs, grinders use scalpels, professional piercers, and a high tolerance for personal risk. The scene sits at the intersection of the body-modification community (which supplies the implantation skill and aftercare norms) and electronics hobbyism (which supplies the payloads).

The mature, low-risk end is **subdermal RFID/NFC transponders** — small glass-encapsulated chips (typically injected in the webbing of the hand) that act as a fixed ID or store small data, readable by phones and access-control readers. This is the most normalized implantable: standardized products exist, professional installers exist, and the failure modes are well understood. Uses are mundane and real: door/computer access, storing a URL or crypto key, replacing a transit/access card. It is not tracking (no GPS, passive, cm-range read).

The higher-risk, more experimental end is **magnet implants** (a small coated magnet in a fingertip that lets you "feel" electromagnetic fields and pick up small ferrous objects — the most reported "genuinely new sense" in the scene), **subdermal LEDs**, temperature/biometric sensors, and larger custom "wetware" devices. These carry real risks: rejection, infection, coating failure (a magnet's biocompatible coating breaching is a serious problem), MRI incompatibility, and the fact that you are performing minor surgery outside a medical system. The scene's ethos is explicitly anti-gatekeeping — the belief that you own your body and don't need institutional permission to modify it — which is both its animating principle and its main risk factor.

Culturally, grinders overlap with `03` (sensory augmentation — the DIY end is literally grinder work) and are culturally adjacent to `04` (medical device security), sharing an "implanted electronics" curiosity and a skeptical stance toward medical/regulatory gatekeeping, while differing completely on who bears the risk (grinders: themselves; med-device security: patients).

## Current state (2026)

Split by risk tier. The **RFID/NFC implant end is mature and stable**: Dangerous Things/VivoKey are established vendors with real products, active community support, and cryptographically capable chips (secure elements for auth, some payment experimentation). This corner is genuinely useful and low-drama — a solved-ish niche technology with a real user base.

The **experimental end (large sensors, LEDs, exotic wetware) is quieter than its 2013–2016 peak.** Grindhouse Wetware's high-profile projects generated a lot of press but little durable product; [verify] the collective's current status is unclear. Magnet implants remain the most popular "new sense" experiment and are widely done, though coating quality and rejection remain issues.

Net: the scene didn't explode into the mainstream the way 2015-era hype implied, but it didn't die either — it settled into a stable niche with a strong practical core (RFID/NFC) and a smaller experimental fringe.

## Red flags / caveats

- **This is surgery.** Even a "simple" injection is a wound with infection risk performed outside a medical system. Magnet-coating failure can cause serious tissue damage. The anti-gatekeeping ethos is exactly where the medical risk lives.
- **MRI incompatibility** — ferrous/metallic implants are an MRI hazard and a lifelong disclosure obligation. Rarely front-of-mind in the marketing.
- **Vendor = primary source problem.** The best technical documentation is also the vendor (Dangerous Things). Their forum is genuinely high-quality, but keep the `[commerce]` frame: capability claims and enthusiasm are not independent.
- **Capability inflation** — implanted chips are passive, short-range, low-storage ID/data devices, not sci-fi. "Chipped = tracked" is false; "chip replaces all your keys/cards seamlessly" is also usually overstated (reader compatibility is finicky).
- **Reversibility** — some implants are hard to remove cleanly. Treat as semi-permanent.
- **Dead-project risk** — several celebrated projects (Circadia, Northstar) were demos more than shipping products; don't mistake a viral demo for available tech.
