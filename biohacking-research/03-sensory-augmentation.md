# 03 — Sensory Augmentation

## What it is

Sensory augmentation is the project of extending existing human senses or adding new ones, by feeding novel information streams into the nervous system. It rests on **sensory substitution** — the well-established neuroscience finding that the brain can learn to interpret arbitrary sensory input through an unusual channel (e.g. "seeing" via patterns of touch on the tongue or torso). Paul Bach-y-Rita's tactile-vision work in the 1960s–70s is the scientific foundation: the brain is the interpreter, and the sensory organ is somewhat interchangeable.

The field spans three culturally distinct wings that share a technical premise. The **clinical/HCI research wing** builds and studies devices — cochlear implants and retinal prostheses are the mature medical end; experimental haptic vests and tongue displays are the research end. The **DIY/grinder wing** overlaps `02`: implanting a fingertip magnet to feel electromagnetic fields, or an implanted compass, is the cheapest real "new sense" available and is done routinely in that scene. The **cyborg-art wing** (Harbisson, Ribas) treats added senses as artistic and identity practice — permanently attached antennas, vibration implants keyed to seismic data — and has been the movement's most visible public face.

The strongest current commercial/technical bet is **haptic sensory substitution via wearables** — turning sound, or other data, into patterns of vibration on the skin, which the brain learns to decode. David Eagleman's academic work and his company NeoSensory (the Buzz wristband, aimed initially at hearing loss/tinnitus) is the flagship example of moving sensory substitution from lab to product. Unlike implants, it's non-invasive and removable, which is why it's the wing most likely to reach ordinary users.

The honest framing: the *principle* (neuroplasticity handles novel input) is solid and decades-proven; the *question* is always whether a given device delivers enough usable bandwidth and training to produce a genuinely functional new sense versus a novelty. Medical sensory restoration (cochlear/retinal) clearly clears that bar; consumer "new sense" devices vary.

## Current state (2026)

Bimodal but real. The **medical/restoration end is genuinely mature**: cochlear implants are routine, and sensory-substitution devices like BrainPort have FDA clearance. The **haptic-substitution consumer end (NeoSensory)** is the most credible attempt to make a general "add a sense" product mainstream, anchored in legitimate neuroscience; watch it as the bellwether for whether consumer sensory augmentation is more than novelty. ## Red flags / caveats

- **Novelty vs. functional sense.** Many devices deliver a *detectable* new input but not enough bandwidth/training to become a *useful* sense. Distinguish "I can tell when a magnet is near" from "I have a working new perceptual channel."
- **Vendor-dependence of implants.** If the company dies, an implanted device can become useless or hazardous (Argus II). Non-invasive/wearable augmentation avoids this; implants don't.
- **Identity/marketing halo.** The cyborg-art wing is compelling and media-friendly, which can inflate perceived technological maturity. Great art ≠ shipping tech.
- **Evidence asymmetry.** Restoration (cochlear/retinal/BrainPort) has clinical evidence; consumer "augmentation" often has founder claims and small studies. Ask for the trial, not the TED talk.
- **`[commerce]` on consumer devices** — NeoSensory, Cyborg Nest are products; evaluate claims accordingly, even where the underlying science is sound.
